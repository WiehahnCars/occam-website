Systems

building is no longer the constraint. judgment is.

26 Jan 2026 • Wiehahn Carstens • 8 min read

Over the past few years, the economics of software creation have changed more dramatically than at any point before. Advances in artificial intelligence, low-code platforms, and automation tooling have collapsed the cost, time, and expertise historically required to build software.

Capabilities that once demanded specialised engineering teams can now be assembled by individuals or small business units in a matter of days. Prototypes, dashboards, and workflows can be created faster than many production systems could be planned just a few years ago. This shift is real, and it is irreversible.

The result is a broad democratisation of development. More actors can now translate intent into software. More ideas can be tested. More systems can be built closer to the operational edge.

That is a meaningful advance.

But it also marks a transition point. When building is no longer scarce, the limiting factor is no longer execution capacity. It is the ability to correctly define the problem worth solving.

This new reality introduces two structural risks.

the paradox of accessibility

As the barrier to building software has fallen, organisations have begun to move directly into implementation before achieving clarity on the problem itself.

Where even a minimal system once required months of planning, design, and coordination, teams can now assemble working prototypes in days or hours. That acceleration feels like progress, but it carries a hidden cost. Many systems are being created without a clear understanding of why they exist or what outcome they are meant to produce.

In practice, decisions about what to build are increasingly driven by available tools rather than by a disciplined articulation of the underlying problem. Technology becomes the starting point instead of the consequence of analysis.

This inversion is subtle, but dangerous. When solutions are defined before the problem is understood, organisations optimise for speed while drifting away from relevance.

None of this yet addresses a deeper issue. Even well intentioned solutions, built quickly and competently, are often disconnected from broader organisational strategy. Alignment between problem selection, system design, and strategic intent is frequently assumed rather than made explicit.

That gap, and its consequences, deserves separate treatment.

superficial fixes and symptom solving

The first consequence of accelerated building is that many solutions address symptoms rather than underlying causes.

Across organisations, a common pattern emerges. There is a sense that something is not working. A visible indicator is identified. A technical solution is assembled quickly, often using AI or low code tools, because the cost of doing so is low. The organisation expects improvement.

In many cases, it does not arrive.

The resulting systems are often fast and technically competent, but shallow. They respond to surface signals rather than structural constraints. Over time, multiple teams produce overlapping solutions, each locally optimised and disconnected from a coherent view of the organisation or its customers.

This creates fragmentation. Workflows diverge. Adoption weakens. Users lose confidence in new tools because they cannot distinguish signal from noise.

The outcome is not transformation, but proliferation. A growing landscape of systems that appear innovative while failing to meaningfully change how decisions are made or outcomes are produced.

when fast turns into debt

The second issue extends beyond poor problem selection. It is the accumulation of technical debt.

Technical debt is the long term cost of short term decisions. It emerges through incomplete architecture, insufficient validation, untested assumptions, and solutions that prioritise speed over durability. Even in more traditional development environments, these choices make systems harder to maintain, adapt, and scale.

The current generation of AI assisted development accelerates this dynamic. Code can be produced faster than it can be fully understood. Prototypes are promoted into production systems without a corresponding increase in rigour. In many teams, a meaningful portion of deployed code is generated or heavily assisted by automated tools, while review and verification practices continue to lag.

The result is fragility. As systems grow and teams evolve, unresolved assumptions accumulate. Dependencies harden. Change becomes expensive and risky.

What begins as acceleration ultimately constrains innovation rather than enabling it.

the real gap: skipping problem definition for tech execution

We’ve seen a consistent pattern emerge across organisations. The work of problem definition is bypassed in favour of immediate technical execution.

As software creation becomes easier than deep analysis, teams allocate disproportionate effort to building and insufficient effort to understanding. Critical steps are compressed or omitted entirely. User needs are inferred rather than examined. Success criteria remain implicit. Non technical alternatives are rarely explored. Problems remain unstructured.

This is where most value is lost.

Without a disciplined approach to defining the problem, technology optimises locally while outcomes degrade globally. The system may function, but it does not resolve what actually matters.

The real leverage lies upstream, in making the problem explicit before attempting to solve it.

why this matters and what the future holds

The democratisation of building tools is a positive and irreversible shift. It allows more people to translate intent into software and lowers the barrier to experimentation across organisations.

At the same time, it changes the nature of the challenge. As building becomes easier, failure modes move upstream.

More solutions will be misaligned with real needs. Technical debt will accumulate faster than it can be resolved. Adoption will suffer as systems proliferate without coherence. Innovation will increasingly feel noisy rather than directional.

The constraint is no longer execution capacity. It is judgment.

Sustained progress now depends on the ability to choose the right problems and define them with precision before committing to technology. Innovation does not begin with code. It begins with clarity.

Our approach is built around this premise. We focus on understanding context, constraints, stakeholders, and intended outcomes before translating intent into technical design and execution.

When the problem is correctly defined, technology becomes an accelerant rather than a liability. Systems solve real issues, technical debt is constrained, adoption improves, and fast tools produce durable value.

In an environment where everyone can build, advantage belongs to those who build with discipline rather than speed alone.

closing thought

We are in the middle of a generational shift in how technology is created. The tools are powerful, the pace is accelerating, and the range of possible solutions continues to expand.

Technology alone will not resolve ambiguity. Clarity, understanding, and disciplined problem definition remain the decisive factors.

The organisations that succeed in this next phase will not be those that move the fastest, but those that can impose structure on complexity and turn capability into coherent action.
